Color deficiency Simulation

  • Organization : Kats Laboratory of Applied Physics
  • Professor : Dr. Mikhail Kats
  • Duration : Ongoing (since December 2017)
  • Technologies : Matlab, Hyperspectral Imaging, Unity, Oculus
  • Description : We design algorithms to perform chromatic adjustment on hyperspectral images to simulate the vision of color vision deficiency (CVD). We then visualize our experiment results in virtual reality to simulate the experience
  • Application : This technique can be used to mathematically recreate the vision of Color Vision Deficiency and provide a realistic simulation through VR. It can also potentially be reverse engineered as a vision enhancement algorithm to allow humans more see more colors

  • Duration : 1 year and 3 months

  • Mimicry Control : In the first half of the researh, I implemented a virtual reality system that allows users to remote control robots with their hand and arm gestures by passing ROS (Robot Operating System) data between Unity and the robot through network socket with little latency. The video belows demonstrate a snippet of what it looks like.

  • Recreate Robot Movement : During the second half of the research, due to COVID-19 restriction, I was not able to be in the lab and research with the robot, so I switched gear into replicating movement by interpolating data from actual robotic experiments in Unity. Binocular Rivalry Through Virtual Reality

  • Organization : KATS LABORATORY OF APPLIED PHYSICS
  • Professor : Dr. Mikhail Kats
  • Duration : 6 months
  • Technologies : Unity, SteamVR
  • Description : The human visual system perceives the world by combining information received from convergence of two eyes and infer three-dimensional cues such as depth. Here, we demonstrate a virtual reality system that breaks the inherent binocular redundancy by projecting two different contents, both dynamically and statically, to each eye.
  • Application : By breaking binocular redundancy in virtual reality, our method aims to provide new ways to customize and simulate binocular rivalry without the need for physical equipment such as a mirror stereoscope. With the foundation of our technique, it should be possible to create more complex and personalized binocular disparities. This technique yields implication to binocular disparity experiments, virtual reality comfort design, and potentially human vision enhancement.
